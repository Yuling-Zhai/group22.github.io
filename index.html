Weapon Laws — Data Story

🔎 Introduction

This website explores weapon-related crimes in San Francisco (2008–2024), examining how crime levels shifted over time and in relation to new legislation like Proposition 63 and Senate Bill 2.

Weapon Laws in crime data include:

  • Possession of illegal weapons
  • Carrying weapons without permits
  • Violation of weapon regulations
  • Felon in possession of a firearm
  • Illegal firearm sales or transfers

Note: These laws do not include weapon use in assault or robbery.

📊 Story in Time: What Happened in These Years?

Between 2008 and 2024, a total of 25,703 Weapon Laws cases were recorded. The following dashboard allows you to explore total counts, trends, and seasonal patterns over time.

🔥 Heatmap: Tracing Weapon Crime Before and After California’s Gun Laws

California’s weapon laws got progressively tougher—Prop 63 in 2016 added ammo restrictions, and SB 2 in 2023 banned guns near schools, parks, and more. But how did these policies actually shape public safety in San Francisco?

We visualized weapon-related crime across four periods:

  • 📍 2012–2015 (Before Prop 63)
    A total of 5,861 cases, mostly concentrated in the southeast (Bayview, Mission).
    Western areas like Richmond and Sunset remained relatively safe.
  • 📍 2017–2020 (After Prop 63)
    Despite the new law, crime increased to 6,090.
    While some southern districts saw minor drops, hotspots like Bayview and Tenderloin stayed intense.
  • 📍 2021–2022 (Pre-SB 2)
    Incidents fell to 3,608, possibly due to pandemic-related shifts.
    Hot areas persisted, but western zones grew even quieter.
  • 📍 2023–2024 (After SB 2)
    Slight rise to 3,402 cases, but spatial patterns shifted:
    Sensitive areas like parks and schools showed some reduction,
    yet hotspots remained, suggesting partial, not full, policy impact.

🔎 What the Map Tells Us

  • ✅ Gun-free zones help, but aren’t a silver bullet
  • ❗ Crime shifted but didn’t vanish—Mission and Bayview still dominate
  • ⚖️ Policies must combine legal reform with community investment to target root causes
“Regulation alone can’t erase the geography of crime.”
— Prof. R. Gonzalez, UC Berkeley.
