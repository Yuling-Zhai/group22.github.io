Introduction

This website explores weapon-related crimes in San Francisco (2008–2024), examining how crime levels shifted over time and in relation to new legislation like Proposition 63 and Senate Bill 2.

  • Possession of illegal weapons
  • Carrying weapons without permits
  • Violation of weapon regulation statutes
  • Felon in possession of a firearm
  • Illegal firearm sales or transfers

Story in Time: What Happened in These Years?

Between 2008 and 2024, a total of 25,703 Weapon Laws cases were recorded.

Yearly Trend: Line chart with red trend and gray dashed events. Hover to view event details.

Monthly Counts: Monthly distribution by year. Red bars highlight peak months.

Seasonal Pattern: January/October often peak; May–August usually drop.

Hourly Trend: High between afternoon and midnight on weekends. Early mornings are quiet.

What's Behind the Trends ?

  • 2008–2011: — AB962 restricted ammunition sales, probably leading to a steady decline in weapon law cases.
  • 2011–2012: — AB 109 reduced prison populations, could indirectly increasing weapon-related offenses.
  • 2012–2013: — The Sandy Hook shooting. Tragic Hallmark in the period
  • 2014–2017: — Prop 47 reduced penalties, probably contributing to more weapon laws cases.
  • 2017: — Prop 63 tightened ammunition and magazine restrictions, effectively reducing weapon law cases.
  • 2018–2019: — SB1100 raised the gun purchase age to 21, lowering youth-involved cases.
  • 2020–2021: — COVID-19, social unrest, and policing challenges could fuel a spike in weapon law cases.
  • 2021–2022: — Post-pandemic tensions and increased gun purchases kept violations elevated.
  • 2022–2024: — AB1594 enhanced gun industry accountability, probably contributing to the recent decrease.

Heatmap: Tracing Weapon Crime Before and After California’s Gun Laws

California’s weapon laws have become stricter over time— Prop 63 (2016) introduced ammunition background checks, and SB 2 (2023) established gun-free zones near schools, parks, and other sensitive locations. But did these laws actually reduce crime in San Francisco?

We visualized weapon-related crimes across four time periods:

2012–2015 (Before Prop 63)

  • Total incidents: 5,861
  • Hotspots in the southeast (Bayview, Mission)
  • Western districts like Richmond and Sunset remained calm

2017–2020 (After Prop 63)

  • Total incidents: 6,090
  • Minor improvements in some areas, but key hotspots persisted
  • New concentration in the Tenderloin district

2021–2022 (Before SB 2)

  • Total incidents: 3,608
  • Citywide decline, possibly due to pandemic effects
  • Hotspots remained, though less intense overall

2023–2024 (After SB 2)

  • Total incidents: 3,402
  • Further decline in total crimes
  • Hot zones like Mission and Bayview still active

What Do These Maps Suggest?

  • SB 2 may be helping, especially near sensitive areas
  • Hotspots remain—policy alone can’t erase deep-rooted crime patterns
  • Community-level strategies are essential alongside legislation
